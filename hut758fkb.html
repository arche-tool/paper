The final orientation map of the reconstructed parent austenite phase is presented in Figure \ref{433657}. Self-evidently, this microstructure is very similar to the experimental results shown in Figure \ref{406044}a, both in terms of grain size/ morphology and crystallographic orientations. A unique aspect of the presented reconstruction method is that unlike the other alternative techniques (e.g. the ARPGE model based on the group properties of ferrite variants of the same parent austenite developed by Cayron et al \cite{cayron2007arpge}) which produces a high number of unsolved points, the algorithm presented in this paper is able to find a unique solution as parent phase orientation for every data points of the product phase. The latter is a consequence of employing the Markov Clustering algorithm for the reconstruction purpose. A cross-correlation based approach was utilized to deterministically validate the reconstructed microstructure. To this purpose, the misorientation angle between corresponding data points in the experimentally obtained and the reconstructed microstructures (i.e. the microstructures presented in Figure \ref{406044}a and Figure \ref{433657}, respectively) was calculated. It should be noted that in spite of the application of the micro-Vickers indentations to fix the EBSD scan area before and after quenching in liquid nitrogen, presence of some misalignments (horizontal and vertical shifts as well as sample rotation) between the two EBSD scans shown in Figure \ref{406044} -and consequently between the microstructures presented in Figure \ref{406044}a and Figure \ref{433657})- was inevitable. To minimize the error caused by this misalignment, a previously developed alignment algorithm \cite{Pirgazi_2019} was used to meticulously align the two above mentioned microstructures before the point-by-point misorientation angle calculation. The alignment technique was originally developed to align the two-dimensional EBSD sections obtained during a three-dimensional EBSD measurement accomplished by a serial sectioning approach \cite{8511652,Pirgazi_2015}. Figure \ref{256149} shows the distribution of the minimum misorientation angle between the reconstructed and the reference microstructures. As it can be seen, the distribution exclusively features two peaks, one with the higher intensity at low misorientation angles (<\(5^{\circ}\)) and the other one at around \(60^{\circ}\).  The high intensity peak at low misorientation angles indicates the good fit between the reconstructed austenite microstructure and the experimentally observed one.  On the other hand, the appearance of the second peak is due to the inefficiency of the algorithm to resolve some of the twin orientations in the reconstructed austenite grains. The missing twins can be easily spotted by a comparison between the microstructures presented in Figure \ref{406044}a and Figure \ref{433657}. Except from some twin orientations, these results show that the presented algorithms can be successfully employed to reproduce detailed microstructural features of the parent austenite grains from the martensitic microstructure.

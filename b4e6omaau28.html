In Equations \ref{eq:1} and \ref{eq:2}, \(T^{\alpha^\prime\leftrightarrow\gamma}\), \(g^{\alpha^\prime}\), \(g^{\gamma}\), \(O\) and \(\Delta g\) are matrices representing the orientation relationship between parent and product phases, orientation matrix of the product phase, orientation matrix of the parent phase, symmetry operator, and angular deviation, respectively. The m and n subscripts correspond to the 24 variants for the OR. In the ideal case, when an OR holds, angular deviation (\(\Delta g\)) in Equations \ref{eq:1} and \ref{eq:2} must be equal to \(0\) . Therefore one can find the best \(T^{\alpha^\prime\leftrightarrow\gamma}\) matrix by minimizing \(\Delta g\) for the grain boundaries. In principle, only grain boundaries in which the OR holds (i.e. where both grains are derived from the same parent grain) should be included in this calculation. Including other boundaries increase the error but since the number of grain boundaries exhibiting the OR is expected to be vastly larger than the number of prior parent grain boundaries (which evidently do not hold the OR), the noise added can be ignored. This is a reasonable assumption since the martensitic phase transformation is accompanied by grain refinement and all the newly generated grain boundaries have misorientations that hold the OR.
Although the search for the existing orientation relationship  (\(T^{\alpha^\prime\leftrightarrow\gamma}\)) could be performed without grain boundary selection (at cost of an increased error), the following technique can be applied to improve this step. By taking advantage of the prior knowledge about the theoretical ORs, one of them (e.g. K-S) can be used to evaluate the initial values of \(\Delta g\). Since the optimized \(T^{\alpha^\prime\leftrightarrow\gamma}\) and the theoretical OR are expected to be close (less than a few degrees of deviation) the grain boundaries which hold the OR should have \(\Delta g\) values near \(0\). Therefore selecting boundaries with  \(\Delta g\) values lower than a threshold (current implementation defaults to \(10^\circ\)) reduces the error of the calculation in a fully automated manner.
There are two alternative methods to calculate grain boundary misorientations \(\Delta g\). One is based on the average orientation of both neighboring grains. The other one is based on the orientation in the immediate vicinity of the grain boundary, where only the first layer of voxels at each side of the grain boundary is selected. Slightly different values of OR are obtained by the two strategies due to plastic strain accommodation during phase transformation and associated orientation gradients in the bulk of the grains \cite{miyamoto2009accurate}.  The presented method of the OR calculation was validated with an Fe-29%Ni alloy. The presented method of the OR calculation was validated with an Fe-29%Ni alloy. Details of this sample and employed heat-treatment have been explained in the experimental procedure section. Figure \ref{200480} compares the distribution of angular component of \(\Delta g\) using the theoretical K-S and the optimized OR. Note that for the optimized OR, the K-S correspondence was used to select grain boundaries and initial value of OR to minimize the error and optimize the search procedure. It can be noticed that when the optimized OR is used, the distribution shifts to the left for lower angular values, i.e. the overall error reduces. Also notice that the distribution shifts to right for higher angular values, resulting in an improved signal/noise relation and making it easier to distinct between boundaries following the OR and prior austenite boundaries. Figure \ref{170946} shows the improvement of the signal/noise ratio resulted from the utilization of the optimized OR applied to the microstructure grid of the Fe-29%Ni alloy. As it can be seen, by using the optimized OR the angular deviation for  the majority of the GBs between the product grains of the same parent reduces (i.e. the dominant green coloured GBs resulted from the K-S correspondence vs the dominant blue coloured GBs resulted from the optimized OR in Figure \ref{170946}).  On the other hand,  replacement of the K-S by the optimized OR increases the angular deviation for the parent phase boundaries and makes them easier to distinguish.

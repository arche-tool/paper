Validation and discussion

Figure \ref{460293}a shows the results of the presented reconstruction method with the optimized OR based on the near-GB orientations (cf. Figure \ref{657394}) after different cycles of iteration. These microstructures are a subset of the reconstructed parent structure which correspond to the marked area in Figure \ref{406044}. The average angular deviation (\(\Delta g\)) for the reconstructed grains have been plotted in Figure \ref{460293}b. As it can be seen, the first iteration results in large austenite grains with relatively high i angular deviation (\(\Delta g\)) for each reconstructed grain into consideration (cf. Figure \ref{460293}b). High angular deviation of the large reconstructed austenite grains indicates the presence of more than one parent grain in the cluster. Only two steps of iterations is enough to quickly refine these grains into smaller austenite grains with sufficiently low angular deviations. However, the algorithm fails to reconstruct some small features of the microstructure (especially crystal twinnings) at this level of refinement (cf. the missed microstructural features marked by arrows in Figure \ref{460293}a, step 3). A careful look at Figure \ref{460293} shows that further iterations of refinement improves the reconstruction of most twins, although some thin twins are still missing in the microstructure. The reconstructed microstructure does not significantly change after 7 steps of iterations, and as more iterations might result in over-refined grains, this stage is considered as the optimum refinement and best reconstruction of austenite microstructure for the studied sample. 
In an actual case, where no knowledge of the parent phase structure is available, the average angular deviation of the reconstructed grains can be employed to select the best number of refinement steps without the risk of over-division of clusters. Figure \ref{698603} plots the changes of the average angular deviation after each step of refinement. As expected, the initial steps (1-3 iterations) significantly improved the angular deviation while the effect of the final steps (+7 iterations) is negligible. In this case, any number of refinement steps between 5and 7will similarly result in the best reconstruction.

Validation and discussion

Figure \ref{460293}a shows the results of the presented reconstruction method with the optimized OR based on the near-GB orientations (cf. Figure \ref{657394}) after different cycles of iteration. These microstructures are a subset of the reconstructed parent structure which correspond to the marked area in Figure \ref{406044}. The average angular deviation (\(\Delta g\)) for the reconstructed grains have been plotted in Figure \ref{460293}b. As it can be seen, the first iteration results in large austenite grains with relatively high angular deviation. The high angular deviation of a reconstructed grain indicates the presence of more than one parent grain in the cluster. Only two steps of iterations is enough to quickly refine these grains into smaller austenite grains with sufficiently low angular deviations. At this step, many delicate microstructural features such as crystal twinnings and small grains have been uncovered and are present in the microstructure. A careful look at Figure \ref{460293} shows that further iterations of refinement slightly improves the angular deviation, however the reconstructed microstructure does not significantly change after 7 steps of iterations, and as more iterations might result in over-refined grains, this stage is considered as the optimum refinement and best reconstruction of austenite microstructure for the studied sample. 
Consequently, the average angular deviation of the reconstructed grains can be considered as appropriate parameter to select the best number of refinement steps without the risk of over-division of clusters. Figure \ref{698603} plots the changes of the average angular deviation after each step of refinement. As expected, the initial steps (1-3 iterations) significantly improved the angular deviation while the effect of the final steps (+7 iterations) is negligible. In this case, any number of refinement steps between 5and 7will similarly result in the best reconstruction.
